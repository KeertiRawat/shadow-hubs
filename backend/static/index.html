Shadow Hubs in Global Oil Trade ▶

This visualization maps bilateral crude and refined petroleum trade flows (UN Comtrade HS 2709/2710) from 2019 to 2024, overlaid with OFAC sanctions exposure and network-derived "shadow hub" scores.

What Are Shadow Hubs? ▶

A shadow hub is a country that is unusually central in the oil trade network relative to its actual trade volume. We measure this using betweenness centrality residuals: if a country sits on many shortest paths between trading partners but doesn't move much oil itself, it has a high shadow residual.

A high shadow residual does not prove illicit activity — it identifies structurally anomalous intermediaries that may warrant closer examination. Legitimate factors (geography, refining capacity, free trade zones) can also produce elevated centrality.

Clustering Analysis ▶

Why clustering coefficients? Shadow hub detection (via betweenness centrality residuals) tells us which countries are anomalous intermediaries — but not how they broker oil. The weighted clustering coefficient answers this by measuring whether a country's trade partners also trade with each other.

The math: For each country i, the weighted clustering coefficient Cw(i) is the fraction of triangles formed among its neighbors, weighted by trade volume. We compute this via nx.clustering(G, weight='log_weight') on the directed trade graph for each year. A triangle exists when country i trades with both j and k, and j also trades with k.

Intuition: Low clustering means a country's partners do not trade with each other — the country is a lone bridge between disconnected groups. High clustering means its partners form a dense clique — oil circulates within a tight network.

Three behavioral patterns emerge:

  • Exclusive Broker (CC < 0.20): Acts as a sole bridge between disconnected trading blocs. Partners rarely trade directly — all flows route through this hub. Example: Singapore (0.18), Turkey (0.19). Consistent with legitimate transshipment hubs or potential sanctions evasion chokepoints.
  • Mixed/Moderate (CC 0.20–0.40): Some partner interconnection but still structurally important as an intermediary. Example: Spain (0.24), Kenya (0.31). May serve regional brokerage roles with partial direct partner trade.
  • Laundering Syndicate (CC > 0.40): Partners trade heavily with each other, forming a dense sub-network. Oil circulates within a tight cluster rather than passing through a single chokepoint. Example: Georgia (0.45), Uzbekistan (0.42). Consistent with coordinated trade rings or regional blocs.

Key insight: High clustering + high shadow residual = strongest signal for organized illicit intermediation. A country that is both structurally anomalous (high betweenness residual) and embedded in a dense trade clique (high clustering) warrants the closest scrutiny.

Key Observations ▶
  • Canada → USA is consistently the largest flow (~$300-450B/yr)
  • Russia's flows drop sharply after 2021 sanctions (213 → 33 by 2024)
  • Iran similarly contracts from 69 flows (2021) to 28 (2024)
  • Shadow hubs like UAE, Turkey, and Singapore show high centrality despite moderate volumes
Data Sources ▶

Analysis period: 2019–2024. All raw and processed data is included in the GitHub repo.

  • UN Comtrade — bilateral trade flows via the comtradeapicall Python package (HS 2709 crude petroleum, HS 2710 refined petroleum)
  • OFAC SDN List — U.S. Treasury Specially Designated Nationals list (sdn.csv, add.csv, sdn_comments.csv)
  • UN/LOCODE — port and location geocoding
  • World Port Index (WPI) — NGA Pub 150, supplementary port coordinates

Network diagnostics (shadow hub residuals, betweenness centrality, clustering coefficients) are computed in the project notebooks using NetworkX.
